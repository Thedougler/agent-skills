# Interview Preparation Guide

## STAR Format

Structure answers as: **Situation** (context), **Task** (your responsibility), **Action** (what you did), **Result** (outcome).

Keep answers to 1-2 minutes. Be specific. End with what you learned or would do differently.

## Ready-Made STAR Examples

### 1. NIC Student Dormitories Project (Large-Scale Execution, Independence)
**S:** Trademark Painting was contracted for the NIC Student Dormitories project — three buildings, four floors, dozens of suites. It was an 8-month commercial painting job requiring multiple techniques.
**T:** I needed to perform the full range of painting work — surface prep, sanding, daxing, taping, priming, rolling, spraying — across all suites, plus detailed wood finishing on exposed structural columns.
**A:** I took on full painter responsibilities with minimal supervision, working systematically through suites and adapting techniques for different surfaces. I handled both broad coverage work (spraying, rolling) and detailed finishing (staining and polishing wood columns).
**R:** Completed the project on schedule across all three buildings. Earned enough trust to be given progressively more responsibility, eventually co-leading crews on subsequent projects.
**Use for:** "Tell me about a large project you completed," "How do you handle working independently," "Describe a time you took on increasing responsibility"

### 2. Courtenay Public Library Roof (Leadership, Initiative)
**S:** After the NIC Dormitories project wrapped up, Trademark Painting took on the restoration and repainting of the Courtenay Public Library roof, followed by the exterior walls.
**T:** I was asked to co-lead a team of labourers on the roof restoration — my first time in a crew leadership role.
**A:** I coordinated the team's work, ensured safety procedures were followed at height, and managed the workflow to keep the project moving efficiently. After the roof was complete, I assisted with the exterior wall repaint.
**R:** Successfully completed the restoration with no safety incidents. The experience demonstrated my readiness for more responsibility and confirmed my ability to lead while still doing hands-on work.
**Use for:** "Describe a time you led a team," "Tell me about a safety-critical situation," "How do you handle new responsibilities"

### 3. Bell Canada Store Growth (Results, Problem-Solving)
**S:** I worked at The Source / Bell Canada in Port Alberni as one of only three employees. The store needed to grow its subscriber base in a small market.
**T:** Beyond standard sales duties, I needed to find ways to retain existing customers and generate new business in a market with limited foot traffic.
**A:** I leveraged my technical knowledge to become the go-to person for after-purchase support, which built trust and generated repeat business. I also used my web design skills to create better product displays when space was limited.
**R:** The store achieved the highest month-over-month growth of any location in BC and earned several company-wide commendations for sales excellence.
**Use for:** "Tell me about a time you exceeded expectations," "How do you approach problem-solving," "Describe a time you used creativity at work"

### 4. Multi-Industry Adaptability (Learning, Flexibility)
**S:** Over my career I've worked across gaming (Chances Rimrock), paint retail (Cloverdale), telecom (Bell/Shaw), tax services (H&R Block), and commercial painting (Trademark, Trinity).
**T:** Each transition required learning new skills, industry knowledge, and workplace norms quickly.
**A:** I approached each new role as a learning opportunity — asking questions, observing experienced colleagues, and applying transferable skills like customer service, organization, and technical problem-solving. In painting, I went from zero trade experience to co-leading crews within a year.
**R:** I've built a broad skill set and consistently earned trust in new environments. My current focus on earning my Red Seal reflects my commitment to deepening expertise once I've found the right fit.
**Use for:** "Why have you changed industries," "How do you handle learning new things," "Tell me about adaptability"

## Common Tough Questions

### "Why did you leave [previous company]?"
> Focus on growth: moved to painting because it offers skilled trade work with a clear path to Red Seal certification. Previous roles taught valuable skills but painting is the career direction I'm committed to.

### "You don't have formal painting credentials yet."
> I'm actively working toward my Red Seal with approximately two years of commercial and residential experience. I hold WHMIS and Fall Protection certifications and have demonstrated the ability to work at a journeyman level with minimal supervision.

### "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"
> Holding my Red Seal certification, possibly running my own crew or specializing in an area like restoration or detailed wood finishing. I want to be a journeyman-level painter who can take on any project.

### "What's your biggest weakness?"
> I can be too independent sometimes — I'll try to figure something out on my own when asking a question would be faster. I've learned to check in more often, especially on new types of work, because a quick question saves hours of rework.

### "Why this company specifically?"
> Customize per company. Must reference: specific projects, company values, market position, or team structure. Never give a generic answer.

## Questions You Should Ask Interviewers

### About the Role
- "What does a typical week look like in this role?"
- "What types of projects does the crew work on most — residential, commercial, industrial?"
- "What would success look like in the first 6 months?"

### About the Team
- "How big is the crew, and how is work divided?"
- "What's the mix of experience levels on the team?"
- "How do you onboard new painters?"

### About Growth
- "Is there support for apprentices working toward Red Seal?"
- "Are there opportunities to specialize (restoration, spray, detailed finishing)?"
- "How do you handle off-season or slow periods?"

### About Culture
- "How would you describe the team culture?"
- "What safety practices does the company prioritize?"
- "What do people who stay long-term here have in common?"

## Phone/Video Interview Tips
- Have STAR examples written out (use this file)
- Keep a glass of water nearby
- Smile when speaking (it changes your tone)
- Ask for clarification if a question is vague
- It's OK to take 5 seconds to think before answering
- End with: "Is there anything else you'd like to know about my background?"

## After the Application (Best Practice)

### Follow-Up Etiquette
- **Don't call to "stand out"** or to learn more about the role post-submission - this risks a negative impression
- If the employer specified a timeline, respect it and wait
- If no timeline was given and significant time has passed (2+ weeks), a brief call to ask about status is acceptable
- If you have genuinely new, relevant information to share, a short follow-up is fine

### Thank-You Notes
- When you receive any update (interview invitation, rejection, or status update), send a brief thank-you message
- Express appreciation for their time and the process
- Keep it short (2-3 sentences)

## Roleplay Guidelines
When the user asks for interview practice:
1. Ask which role/company to simulate
2. Start with easy warm-up questions ("Tell me about yourself")
3. Progress to role-specific technical questions
4. Include 1-2 behavioral questions using the competencies from the job posting
5. End with a tough question or curveball
6. After each answer, give brief feedback: what worked, what to sharpen
7. Suggest which STAR example would work best for each question
